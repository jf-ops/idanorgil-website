# Web Copywriting

This skill covers writing and editing copy for personal websites and professional service pages. The goal is always the same: clear, specific, human text that earns trust and moves visitors to act.

## Core Principles

### 1. Lead with the reader, not yourself

The most common mistake on personal and professional sites: opening with "I am…" and listing credentials. Readers arrive asking "what's in it for me?" Answer that first.

**Weak:** "I am a licensed psychologist with 15 years of experience in cognitive behavioral therapy."

**Strong:** "Struggling with anxiety, depression, or a life transition? CBT-based therapy that fits your schedule — online and in-person."

Switch "I" to "you" wherever possible. Describe outcomes and benefits before credentials.

### 2. Be specific

Specificity builds trust. Vague language sounds like every competitor.

| Weak | Strong |
|---|---|
| "Experienced professional" | "15 years working with adolescents and young adults" |
| "Comprehensive services" | "Assessment, diagnosis, and weekly therapy sessions" |
| "High-quality results" | "Most clients see measurable progress within 8 sessions" |
| "Convenient location" | "5 minutes from Nørreport station, free parking" |

### 3. Write at a 7th-grade reading level

Professional audiences still prefer simple language. Use:
- Short sentences (under 20 words)
- Active voice ("We help you" not "Help is provided")
- Plain words ("use" not "utilize", "help" not "facilitate")
- One idea per paragraph

### 4. Every page has one primary goal

Ask: what is the single most important action a visitor should take on this page?
- Homepage → understand what you offer + click into services or contact
- About → build trust + click contact or services
- Services page → understand what you offer + book/contact

Remove copy that doesn't serve that goal.

## Page-by-Page Guidance

### Homepage

**Structure:**
1. **Hero headline** — what you do + who it's for, in one sentence
2. **Sub-headline** — the key benefit or differentiator
3. **CTA** — one primary button ("Book a consultation", "See services")
4. **Brief intro** — 2–3 sentences on approach or values
5. **Services overview** — 3–6 tiles or bullets
6. **Trust signals** — credentials, associations, or a client quote
7. **Secondary CTA** — repeat the primary CTA

**Hero headline formula:** `[Outcome] for [Audience]` or `[Service] that [Benefit]`

Examples:
- "Therapy for teens and young adults in Copenhagen"
- "Assessment and treatment that moves at your pace"

Keep headlines under 10 words. Avoid clever wordplay on first read — clarity first.

### About Page

About pages are often the most-visited page on professional service sites. Visitors decide here whether to trust you.

**Structure:**
1. **Opening hook** — why you do this work (not a resume)
2. **Approach** — your philosophy or method in plain language
3. **Background** — education, experience, specializations
4. **Personal touch** — one human detail (optional but builds connection)
5. **CTA** — "Ready to talk? Book a consultation."

**Opening hook approaches:**
- A defining moment or belief: "I became a psychologist because I spent years not understanding my own anxiety. I know what it feels like to sit on the other side."
- A direct statement of purpose: "My work is built on one belief: people change when they feel safe enough to be honest."

Avoid leading with "I grew up in…" or listing degrees in the first paragraph.

### Services Page

**For each service:**
1. Name (clear, not jargon)
2. Who it's for (specific person or situation)
3. What happens / what it involves
4. What changes / the outcome
5. Practical details (duration, format, price if shown)

**Example:**
> **Individual Therapy**
>
> For adults navigating anxiety, depression, burnout, or life transitions. Sessions are 50 minutes, once per week, and can be held in person or online. We work together to understand what's driving the difficulty — then build practical tools to address it. Most clients begin weekly sessions and taper as they feel more steady.

### Contact Page

Keep it short. Remove friction. Include:
- A warm, one-sentence intro ("I'd love to hear from you.")
- The contact form or email address
- Response time expectation ("I reply within 1 business day.")
- Any intake process details ("After your message, I'll suggest a 15-minute phone call.")

## CTAs (Calls to Action)

**Principles:**
- One primary CTA per page
- Describe what happens next, not just the action: "Book a free 15-minute call" beats "Contact me"
- Match the commitment level to where the visitor is: warm visitors → "Book a session"; cold visitors → "Book a free intro call"

**CTA copy options by warmth:**
- Cold: "Learn more", "See how it works", "Read about my approach"
- Warm: "Book a free consultation", "Get in touch", "Start the conversation"
- Ready: "Book a session", "Schedule online", "Make an appointment"

## Tone Guide

For professional service sites (therapy, healthcare, legal, financial):
- **Warm but professional** — never clinical, never overly casual
- **Direct without being pushy** — state what you offer clearly; don't oversell
- **Reassuring** — acknowledge the difficulty of reaching out ("Asking for help takes courage.")
- **Human** — first person, complete sentences, real language

Avoid:
- Jargon without explanation ("CBT", "EMDR", "psychoeducation" — briefly explain on first use)
- Superlatives with no evidence ("the best", "leading", "top")
- Passive voice ("Services are provided" → "I provide")
- Filler phrases ("In today's fast-paced world…", "I am passionate about…")

## Editing Checklist

Before finalizing copy, check:

- [ ] Does the opening sentence serve the reader, not the writer?
- [ ] Is there one clear CTA per page?
- [ ] Are all credentials and claims specific (not vague)?
- [ ] Is every sentence under 25 words?
- [ ] Are jargon terms explained on first use?
- [ ] Does the bio feel human, not like a LinkedIn profile?
- [ ] Is contact information easy to find?
- [ ] Does the copy answer: "Why should I choose you over others?"
